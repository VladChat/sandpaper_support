Product Overview

Support guide for choosing and using the 9 x 11 inch silicon carbide sandpaper sheets in the assorted 60 through 3000 grit kit. The sheets can be used wet or dry depending on the surface and sanding stage.

What Each Grit Range Is For

80-120 Removal / Rough Sanding

Use for heavier removal, rough shaping, or starting on a surface with visible defects. Move finer before finish prep.

150-240 Surface Preparation

Use for general prep after rough sanding, wood smoothing, paint prep, or removing the previous coarse scratch pattern.

280-400 Fine Prep

Use for finer prep, light scuffing, and preparing many surfaces before coating or the next refinement stage.

500-800 Extra Fine Finishing

Use for extra fine finishing and light refinement when the surface already has an even scratch pattern.

1000-3000 Wet Sanding / Polishing Preparation

Use wet for fine refinement, haze reduction, clear coat work, and polishing preparation. Sanding alone does not create full gloss.

How To Choose The Starting Grit

  1. Start with the least aggressive grit that still changes the problem.
  2. If the surface has heavy defects, start in the 80-120 range, then refine step by step.
  3. If the surface only needs preparation, start around 150-240.
  4. If the surface is already smooth and only needs light scuffing or fine prep, start around 280-400.
  5. If the goal is fine wet sanding or polishing preparation, use 1000-3000 after the surface is already level.
Avoid: Do not use 1000-3000 grit to remove heavy defects. High grits refine scratches; they do not replace the earlier cutting steps.

Common Mistakes

Starting Too Fine

Fine grits polish or refine. They are slow when the surface still has raised defects, rust, thick paint, or deep scratches.

Skipping Too Far

Large jumps can leave the previous grit scratches behind. Step down gradually until the old scratch pattern is gone.

Expecting 3000 To Create Gloss

3000 grit leaves a very fine sanding haze. Gloss usually requires the correct next finishing or polishing step.

Using Heavy Pressure

Heavy pressure can create deep scratches, low spots, edge damage, heat on plastic, or early sheet wear.

Letting Dust Or Slurry Stay Under The Sheet

Loose grit, packed dust, or dirty slurry can create random scratches and make the sheet stop cutting cleanly.

FAQ

Can the sheets be used wet?

Yes. The kit is for wet or dry use. Wet sanding is usually for finer stages where water helps control residue and the surface is suitable for wet sanding.

Which grit should I start with?

Start with the least aggressive grit that still removes the defect. If you are unsure, test a less aggressive grit first.

Should I use every grit in the kit?

No. Use the grits needed for the surface and defect. Do not skip too far when visible scratches still remain.

Does 3000 grit make a surface glossy?

No. 3000 grit can prepare a surface for polishing, but sanding alone normally leaves a fine haze.

What size are the sheets?

The sheets are 9 x 11 inches.

What abrasive is used?

The sheets use silicon carbide abrasive.
